# Economics

This document sketches the economic mechanics of the framework: what money does, what happens to existing wealth, how the planetary dividend works, what role firms play, and what work means when survival is decoupled from employment.

**This document is a skeleton, not a finished specification.** The author of the initial draft is a developer, not an economist. The mechanisms here are first-pass proposals. Real economists, political theorists, and practitioners are the intended audience for filling in the details — see [OPEN_QUESTIONS.md](OPEN_QUESTIONS.md) for the specific decisions that need deliberation.

## 1. Money and the two-tier economy

### The proposal

**Two tiers of economic activity, with different rules:**

**Basic tier — decommodified.** Food sufficient for nutrition, shelter adequate for dignity, healthcare, education, basic energy, clean water, basic connectivity, basic mobility. These are provided through Layer 2 — the coordinator — and do not require money to access. Either delivered directly, or funded through a floor-level allocation that ensures universal access.

**Discretionary tier — normal money economy.** Everything above the basic floor operates in a market economy with money, prices, wages, profits, savings, and investment. People choose what to buy, what to make, what to work on. Markets allocate. Prices contain truth because externalities are priced in from Layer 2.

### Why this shape

Because it matches what actually works. Most functioning countries already decommodify some basic goods — healthcare in most of Europe, education through some level, public transit in many cities. The framework extends a principle that has already been validated at smaller scales.

Full decommodification (no money anywhere) kills the discretionary economy's ability to coordinate diverse preferences, reward local knowledge, and generate variety. Full commodification (money for everything) means survival depends on employability, which gives employers coercive power over workers and makes labor markets fundamentally unfree.

Two-tier preserves the virtues of markets where they work while removing the coercive dependency that makes them dysfunctional at the level of basic survival.

### What this does to labor

If food, shelter, healthcare, and education are guaranteed, the answer to "would you rather keep this job or lose it?" changes fundamentally. Workers gain real bargaining power. Exploitative work either improves conditions or disappears. Care work, creative work, community work, and other chronically underpaid activities become viable without wage subsidy.

This is the core freedom the framework aims to deliver: the freedom to say no.

### What this does to consumption

Total aggregate consumption shifts. Basic consumption stabilizes at a high floor (much higher than current baseline for billions of people, modestly higher or comparable for developed-world baseline). Discretionary consumption is free to expand or contract based on actual preferences rather than status competition and anxiety-driven spending.

The demand externality problem from the Hemenway Falk paper is substantially addressed: aggregate demand has a structural floor that cannot collapse, because basic provision is decommodified and therefore independent of the labor market.

## 2. Wealth and capital

### The problem

Existing concentrated wealth is both a moral concern (extreme inequality) and a structural threat to the framework (concentrated capital will resist any system that threatens its prerogatives). The framework cannot ignore this question.

### The proposed path: gradual dissolution through taxation

Not expropriation. Not confiscation. Taxation calibrated to dissolve concentration over a generation:

- **High wealth taxes** at threshold levels, rising steeply with concentration. Piketty's proposal in *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* is the nearest existing analog.
- **Estate taxes approaching 100%** above a threshold. Concentrated wealth dissolves at each generational transfer rather than compounding. Below the threshold, normal inheritance continues — families can pass on homes, businesses, modest savings.
- **No tax havens.** This is where Layer 2's planetary scope becomes critical. The coordinator enforces cross-border financial transparency; there is no jurisdiction to flee to. This has been the missing enforcement mechanism for every wealth-tax proposal in history.

### Why gradual

Because expropriation invites civil war, and civil war kills frameworks. Historical revolutions that moved fast on existing wealth — French, Russian, Chinese — produced massive social trauma and often ended in authoritarian consolidation precisely because the concentrated resistance required authoritarian response.

Gradual dissolution through legible rules, applied universally, with no individual's existing assets seized but with concentrated concentration *over time* prevented, is both more defensible and more durable.

### What happens to corporate capital

Firms continue to exist and operate (see section 4 below). Ownership of firms is subject to the same wealth-concentration limits as any other asset — you can own a business, but above a threshold you cannot own a controlling stake without taking on public-interest obligations. Large firms evolve toward mixed public-private, worker-owned, or cooperative structures over a generation. Small and medium firms operate normally in the free middle.

## 3. The planetary dividend

### What it is

Every human alive receives an equal share of the revenue generated by Layer 2's externality pricing and planetary fund. Citizenship-agnostic. Age-agnostic (children's dividends go into custodial accounts, released at adulthood). Delivered in a medium usable in the recipient's local economy.

### Two streams

**Floor stream — guaranteed minimum.** A baseline dividend calibrated, in combination with decommodified basic provision, to guarantee a dignified life anywhere on Earth. This stream is stable and predictable, functioning like a universal basic income except it's framed and legally structured as *dividend from planetary commons*, not *welfare from state*.

The framing matters. A dividend is your share of something you own. Welfare is a transfer contingent on worthiness. The psychological and political difference is enormous.

**Share stream — variable.** Whatever additional revenue the planetary fund generates, divided by population, distributed on top of the floor. This fluctuates with fund performance. It gives every human a direct material stake in planetary productivity, including AI-driven productivity gains that would otherwise flow entirely to capital.

### Funding sources

The planetary fund is capitalized from:

- **Pigouvian prices** on all major externalities: carbon, biodiversity loss, freshwater depletion, labor displacement from automation, attention extraction, data harvesting, pollution in all forms. These are the primary ongoing revenue source.
- **AI productivity capture.** Frontier AI models and their productivity gains are partially socialized — a share flows to the planetary fund rather than entirely to private owners. The Hemenway Falk paper's finding that "better AI" worsens the demand externality under current structures makes this especially important: capturing AI gains for the dividend neutralizes the effect by redistributing the productivity directly.
- **Land value capture** at appropriate scales. The Georgist tradition — taxation of unimproved land value — is resource-efficient and hard to evade, and captures the social creation of location value that currently flows to private landowners.
- **Extraordinary profits** from natural resource extraction, financial intermediation rents, and monopoly positions.

### What the dividend does economically

Reverses the demand-destruction dynamic. Guarantees a universal consumption floor. Redistributes from concentrated capital holders to the population as a whole. Gives everyone a direct stake in planetary wellbeing (because the fund performs better when ecosystems, economies, and populations are healthier). Makes work genuinely optional at the survival level.

It also, critically, has an exit effect on bad actors: a worker in a coercive workplace, a spouse in an abusive marriage, a citizen in a dysfunctional region — all of them have material resources to leave. Exit options change power dynamics across the entire economy and society.

## 4. Firms, by size

The framework treats firm size as a phase transition — different rules apply at different scales, because market dynamics that work at small scale fail at large scale.

### Small firms (below threshold A)

Operate normally. Profit, compete, hire, innovate, fail. No special obligations beyond paying the externality prices set by Layer 2. This is most business activity: restaurants, local services, workshops, small manufacturers, independent professionals, small agricultural operations, local retail.

The threshold here should be meaningful — enough that the vast majority of businesses operate without additional obligation. Early draft: revenue and employment thresholds calibrated to cover businesses up to roughly regional scale.

### Medium firms (threshold A to threshold B)

**Chartered firms.** Above threshold A, firms take on explicit obligations:

- **Transparency requirements.** Financials, supply chains, ownership, environmental impact.
- **Worker representation** on governance bodies. German codetermination is the nearest analog; roughly equal worker and owner representation on boards above a threshold.
- **Commons-stewardship duty.** Legal fiduciary obligation to the commons, not just to shareholders. This is a real legal change that redefines fiduciary duty.
- **Public-interest obligations** proportional to scale: contribution to local economies, compliance with regional economic plans where they exist, participation in training and apprenticeship systems.

Chartered firms can still be privately owned, profit-making, and competitive. They take on obligations proportional to the public interest they affect.

### Large firms (above threshold B)

**Public utilities or mixed ownership.** If a firm is load-bearing for civilization — critical infrastructure, cloud computing at scale, frontier AI, major financial intermediation, pharmaceuticals at scale, major logistics, major energy — it cannot be purely private.

Options include:

- Regulated public utility (existing model for electricity, water in many jurisdictions)
- Public ownership with professional management (Norway's Statoil, Singapore's Temasek are functional examples)
- Worker-owned at scale (Mondragón model)
- Mixed ownership with mandatory public stake

The specific form is a design decision for each sector. The principle is that at civilizational scale, ownership cannot be fully private — the commons at stake is too large.

### Why size thresholds

Because size changes what a firm *is*. A small restaurant participating in a neighborhood food economy is a very different entity from a multinational agribusiness controlling grain supply chains across continents. Treating them under the same legal framework, with the same rules, was a category error that produced a century of pathology. Phase transitions in legal treatment as firms cross size thresholds reflect the phase transitions in what firms actually are.

## 5. Work

### The question

If the basic livelihood floor is guaranteed through Layer 2, and AI and automation can handle an increasing share of economically valuable work, what do humans do?

### The honest answer

No one knows. This is genuinely one of the framework's weakest points, because it's downstream of changes we haven't lived through yet. The framework's job is to *protect the option space* rather than prescribe a solution.

### What the framework does

**Decouples work from survival.** The livelihood floor means no one has to work to eat. This is the foundational shift; most subsequent changes follow from it.

**Preserves work as an option and a reward.** People who want to work, earn, accumulate (within wealth limits), and build things can do so. Compensation exists. Labor markets exist. The discretionary economy remains.

**Compensates what currently goes uncompensated.** Care work, community work, art, teaching, environmental restoration, parenting, eldercare — all of these can be supported through direct compensation from the planetary fund or through the discretionary economy, rather than being treated as pre-market background activity.

**Pluralizes status.** Humans need recognition and hierarchy; frameworks that try to abolish status fail. The framework pluralizes it: craft mastery, scholarship, caregiving, teaching, organizing, creating, healing, competing in sport — all become viable status economies alongside wealth. This is how most pre-capitalist societies worked; the novelty is achieving it at scale in a technologically advanced society.

**Protects weirdness.** The framework deliberately does not specify what people should do with their time. Religious life, artistic life, experimental communities, research, exploration, craft traditions, new forms of living — all are explicitly within the free middle layer and outside the coordinator's scope. Humans figure out post-labor culture; the framework holds the space for that figuring out.

### What this looks like day-to-day

Speculatively: fewer hours worked on average, but by choice rather than underemployment. More people involved in care, education, community, and creative work, paid enough to make it viable. Firms competing not for labor supply (because coercive labor is no longer a thing) but for meaningful contribution. Local economies more vibrant because people have time and resources for local participation. A longer transition period during which existing identities, skills, and communities adapt — with significant social support during that adaptation.

This is not utopia. It is a set of conditions under which humans can figure out what comes next without being forced into specific answers by survival pressure.

## 6. Open questions

The mechanisms above are first-pass proposals. Several decisions require specialist deliberation. See [OPEN_QUESTIONS.md](OPEN_QUESTIONS.md) for the full list, but the economics-specific ones include:

- Exact level of the livelihood floor and decommodification boundaries
- Specific threshold levels for firm categorization
- Wealth and estate tax calibration
- Transition mechanics for existing concentrated wealth
- Interaction between national currencies and any planetary-scale accounting
- How the dividend handles mobility, migration, and citizenship edge cases
- Whether monetary policy remains national or becomes part of Layer 2
- Treatment of existing sovereign debt, pension systems, and social insurance
- Handling of non-human value (ecosystems, species, future generations) in the fund's accounting
- Labor-time vs. monetary accounting for specific domains

These are not gaps in the framework. They are the points where the framework invites specialist contribution.
