# Transition

How does this become real?

Not through persuasion. Not through winning elections. Not through convincing financial elites to accept a diminished role.

Through being the coherent alternative that is lying around when the current system's failures force a reckoning.

## The Friedman precedent

Milton Friedman, 1982 preface to *Capitalism and Freedom*:

> Only a crisis — actual or perceived — produces real change. When that crisis occurs, the actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying around. That, I believe, is our basic function: to develop alternatives to existing policies, to keep them alive and available until the politically impossible becomes the politically inevitable.

This is exactly what happened. Friedman, Hayek, and the Mont Pelerin Society spent the 1950s and 1960s in relative political wilderness, developing the neoliberal framework, building the institutions (Chicago School, Mont Pelerin, Atlas Network, Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute, American Enterprise Institute), and training the personnel who would be ready to staff a new order when crisis came. The 1970s stagflation crisis arrived. The Keynesian consensus had no coherent response. The neoliberal framework was the only fully-developed alternative on the shelf. Reagan, Thatcher, Deng, and subsequent leaders implemented it globally over the following two decades.

This was not an accident of persuasion. It was the result of thirty years of deliberate preparation by a minority of serious thinkers who understood that their moment would come when the existing consensus failed, and that they needed to be ready.

**This framework operates on the same logic, from the opposite direction.**

## The predicted crisis

The specific triggers cannot be named with certainty, but the structural pressures are visible. Any of these, or their combination, could produce the crisis window:

**Mass automation-driven unemployment.** The Hemenway Falk paper's prediction generalized: as AI and robotics displace workers across sectors faster than new work is created, the demand-destruction dynamic produces economic contraction simultaneously with rising unemployment. Historical analogs (1930s) suggest this can destabilize political systems rapidly.

**Climate-driven displacement and instability.** Climate migration, agricultural disruption, and increasingly frequent disasters strain existing political institutions. The coordination failures visible in current climate response are expected to worsen.

**Financial system fragility.** Concentrated ownership, shadow banking, and increasingly decoupled asset prices from productive capacity produce recurring crises. Each has been managed through extraordinary monetary intervention that has diminishing effectiveness.

**Epistemic collapse.** AI-generated content, attention-capture media, and declining institutional trust may reach a point where shared reality becomes difficult enough that political coordination through existing mechanisms fails.

**Geopolitical realignment.** The unraveling of the US-led international order without a clear replacement produces coordination gaps that create openings for new frameworks.

**Pandemic or bioengineered crisis.** Another large-scale biological event, likely worse than COVID-19, would test institutions that have not fundamentally improved since.

**Simultaneous crises.** The most likely scenario is not one of these alone but several together, producing a window where existing institutions visibly fail and populations look urgently for alternatives.

The framework does not predict *when*. It prepares for *whenever*.

## What readiness looks like

When the crisis arrives, the framework will not be adopted by being read cold by a head of state. It will be adopted because:

- **Multiple versions of it exist and have been debated.** The repository is not the only expression; other thinkers, groups, and communities have engaged with it, disagreed with parts, contributed alternatives. A living intellectual ecosystem exists.
- **Specific policy components have been piloted.** Parts of the framework are tested at smaller scales: regional planetary dividends, city-scale citizen assemblies, open instruction sets for government AI systems, externality pricing regimes, wealth-tax enforcement across cooperating jurisdictions.
- **Personnel are available.** Economists, technologists, political theorists, administrators, and organizers who understand the framework deeply enough to implement it under pressure. This is what think tanks do; the framework needs analogous infrastructure.
- **Institutions have started to use fragments.** Before the crisis, some international body — UN agency, regional bloc, scientific body — has adopted one or another component, creating precedent and operational knowledge.
- **Publics have been exposed.** Not converted, but *aware*. When crisis creates demand for alternatives, "I've heard of this" is much more actionable than cold presentation.

This is a decades-long preparation. Starting now is the point.

## Adoption pathways

Several routes, not mutually exclusive:

### 1. Planetary commons coordination

The cleanest entry point. Problems where states have demonstrably failed to coordinate and where the need is obvious:

- **Climate.** The coordinator begins as a scientifically grounded, transparent successor to the UNFCCC process. Its authority starts advisory and grows as its proposals outperform negotiated alternatives.
- **Pandemic response.** Post-COVID, there is widespread acknowledgment that existing mechanisms failed. A transparent coordination layer for biological threats has a legible adoption case.
- **AI governance itself.** This is uniquely suited: the problem is genuinely planetary, existing governance is nearly non-existent, and there is serious interest across jurisdictions in some form of coordination. A federated, transparent, open-stack AI governance body may be the first actual instantiation of Layer 2.
- **Ocean and fisheries.** The tragedy-of-the-commons case in its original form. Existing treaties have failed. Room for a real coordination layer.

The framework's adoption at this level does not require dismantling any state or existing institution. It requires building a layer above them that handles what they cannot.

### 2. Regional and municipal pilots

Sub-national jurisdictions have historically been where new frameworks first operationalize. Workable pilots:

- **Livelihood floors** at municipal or regional scale. There are already experiments (Alaska, various UBI pilots, Kenya's GiveDirectly programs). Scaling these up toward full decommodified basic provision is incremental, measurable, and reversible if it fails.
- **Citizen assemblies** on specific contested questions. Ireland, France, Belgium have already demonstrated this works for defined questions. Extending them to economic policy is a natural next step.
- **Participatory budgeting** at municipal scale. Already practiced in Porto Alegre, Paris, New York, and hundreds of other cities. Expansion and integration with other framework components is feasible.
- **Cooperative economy zones.** Regions where cooperative and worker-owned firms receive support and infrastructure, generating comparative data on their performance.
- **Open AI governance at city or regional scale.** Municipal AI systems operating on public instruction sets, with citizen oversight.

### 3. Institutional infiltration

Existing institutions adopt framework components one at a time. This is the slow, reformist path, but it builds operational knowledge and precedent:

- **Central banks** incorporate planetary boundary considerations and commons-stewardship duties into mandates. (The Bank of England has already moved in this direction.)
- **Sovereign wealth funds** and pension funds adopt commons-stewardship criteria. This is underway in a fragmented form already.
- **Antitrust authorities** shift from consumer-welfare standards to structural competition standards with explicit commons protection. (Lina Khan's FTC in 2021-2024 demonstrated the political feasibility of some of this.)
- **International financial institutions** (IMF, World Bank) adopt framework-consistent policies in their lending and technical assistance. Mariana Mazzucato's work has already shifted discourse in this direction.

### 4. Counter-institution building

In parallel with the above, new institutions purpose-built for the framework:

- **A public research and policy institute** at the scale of Mont Pelerin or Brookings, explicitly developing and promoting framework components. Funded by aligned philanthropy, ideally geographically distributed.
- **Open-source infrastructure** for the coordinator stack: reference implementations of AI systems operating on public instruction sets, tooling for citizen assemblies, open externality-pricing models.
- **Training programs** for the personnel who will implement the framework. Universities, fellowships, practical experience programs.
- **A global network** of organizations working on framework components, coordinating quietly but substantially. Not a single organization, but an ecosystem.

### 5. Crisis response

When the actual crisis hits, none of the above matters unless the framework is *usable* in emergency conditions. This means:

- **Pre-drafted policy packages** ready to deploy for common crisis types (financial, climate, pandemic, unemployment).
- **Communication materials** translating the framework into publicly accessible language at the moment it's needed.
- **Networks of trained personnel** who can staff emergency coordination bodies.
- **Tested mechanisms** — nothing untested should be deployed in crisis, because failure in crisis produces backlash that discredits everything associated.

The goal is that when some major institution asks "what do we do now?", multiple versions of the answer are available, staffed, and ready.

## What adoption does *not* require

**Does not require convincing current elites.** Current concentrated-wealth holders and their political operatives are a known adversary. The framework will be opposed by them, and this is built into expectations. Adoption happens when their power to block it is weakened by crisis, not when they agree.

**Does not require majority popular support initially.** Major structural changes have generally been implemented by coherent minorities during crisis windows, not majorities in calm periods. Majority support tends to follow successful implementation rather than precede it. What is needed is *enough* support among *enough* of the right people at *the right moment*.

**Does not require simultaneous global adoption.** The framework can start in one region, one commons, one component. It grows from working examples.

**Does not require ideological purity.** The framework is designed to be compatible with multiple political traditions. Social democrats, democratic socialists, cooperativists, ecologists, some strands of conservatism (Burkean concern for commons and institutions), religious traditions concerned with stewardship — all can find themselves in this framework. Broad coalition is both possible and necessary. The enemy is specifically the unpriced-externality, concentrated-capital structure, not any particular political identity.

## Countervailing dynamics and risks

Honesty requires naming what will oppose adoption:

- **Concentrated capital will fund opposition.** Think tanks, media, academic positions, and political campaigns will be deployed against the framework. This happened to neoliberalism too in reverse during the postwar consensus; it was overcome through sustained counter-effort.
- **Existing institutions will protect their prerogatives.** Even institutions nominally aligned with framework goals may oppose frameworks that diminish their role.
- **Authoritarian movements will offer competing "alternatives."** In crisis, authoritarian frameworks are often more operationally ready than democratic ones because they are simpler to describe and deploy. The framework competes in a marketplace of alternatives; it does not have the field to itself.
- **Co-optation risk.** Powerful actors may adopt framework language while gutting substance. This has happened to every emancipatory framework in history and will happen to this one. Vigilance about substance vs. rhetoric is ongoing.
- **First-mover punishment.** Jurisdictions that adopt first may be punished economically or politically by jurisdictions that don't. Layer 2's planetary scope is supposed to eventually solve this, but it doesn't solve it at the beginning. Early adopters need coalitional protection.

These are not reasons not to try. They are reasons to be strategic.

If you find the diagnosis and vision compelling:

- Contribute to the framework's development — open issues, propose changes, translate, criticize.
- Build the parallel institutions in whatever domain you operate in.
- Pilot specific components where you have authority or influence.
- Train yourself and others for the implementation that will be needed.
- Build networks of aligned actors across domains and geographies.
- Be ready for the crisis window when it comes.

The crisis will come. Whether a coherent framework is available when it does is the question this repository exists to answer.
