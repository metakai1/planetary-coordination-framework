# Precedents

This framework builds on work by many others. Nothing in it is wholly original; the novelty is in the combination and in specific proposals around AI-coordinated governance. This document names what it draws on, so contributors and critics can trace ideas to sources and engage with their full context.

Organized by which part of the framework each influences.

## The diagnosis

**Hemenway Falk, B. and Tsoukalas, G. (2026). "The AI Layoff Trap." arXiv:2603.20617.**
The paper that catalyzed this project. Formal demonstration that competitive firms rationally over-automate even with perfect foresight, because each captures cost savings but bears only a fraction of demand destruction. Shows that UBI, capital income taxes, worker equity, and Coasian bargaining all fail to correct it; only a Pigouvian automation tax does. The core insight — that demand itself is a commons being depleted by competitive dynamics — generalizes throughout the framework.

**Philippon, T. (2019). *The Great Reversal.***
Documents the collapse of competitive market structure across most US sectors. Essential grounding for the "scale ate the small-producer assumption" argument.

**Piketty, T. (2014). *Capital in the Twenty-First Century.* (2020). *Capital and Ideology.***
The long-run history of wealth concentration and the tax-based proposals for dissolution that influence the framework's wealth-tax design.

**Mazzucato, M. (2013). *The Entrepreneurial State.* (2018). *The Value of Everything.***
Reshapes the account of where innovation and value actually come from, demolishing the pure-private-sector mythology that underlies much current policy.

**Gilens, M. and Page, B. (2014). "Testing Theories of American Politics." *Perspectives on Politics.***
The most-cited empirical finding that US policy outcomes track elite rather than median-voter preferences. The framework's "democracy isn't currently functioning" claim rests on this kind of evidence.

**Korten, D. (1995). *When Corporations Rule the World.***
Named explicitly by the author as foundational to the worldview. The structural critique of corporate power and its incompatibility with democracy.

**Starr, P. (1982). *The Social Transformation of American Medicine.***
The Pulitzer-winning account of how Rockefeller and Carnegie foundation funding reshaped American medicine. Grounds the "epistemic capture" argument in documented history rather than speculation.

**MacLean, N. (2017). *Democracy in Chains.***
The funded project to remake American political economy that produced neoliberalism. Essential for understanding both the precedent (preparing ideas in advance of crisis) and the adversary (concentrated wealth's ability to shape political outcomes).

**Haidt, J. (2024). *The Anxious Generation.*** and **Twenge, J. (2017). *iGen.***
The adolescent mental health collapse coincident with smartphones and social media. Grounds the cognitive-capture argument.

**Putnam, R. (2000). *Bowling Alone.***
The decades-long measurement of social-fabric decline.

**Desmet, M. (2022). *The Psychology of Totalitarianism.***
On how atomized, anxious populations become manipulable. Controversial but widely read.

**Arendt, H. (1951). *The Origins of Totalitarianism.***
The classic account of the social preconditions for authoritarian capture.

## Alternative economic frameworks

**Raworth, K. (2017). *Doughnut Economics.***
The most direct precursor to the framework's Layer 1 structure. Ecological ceiling and social foundation as bounds on economic activity. The framework's treatment of these as binding constraints rather than aspirations extends Raworth's core insight.

**Cockshott, P. and Cottrell, A. (1993). *Towards a New Socialism.***
Demonstrates that the socialist calculation problem is computationally tractable with modern technology. Essential for the framework's claim that Hayek's calculation argument is historically contingent and technologically obsolete.

**Saros, D. (2014). *Information Technology and Socialist Construction.***
Extends Cockshott and Cottrell with more modern computational and informational frameworks.

**Bastani, A. (2019). *Fully Automated Luxury Communism.***
Post-scarcity as political horizon. Influences the framework's premise that abundance is technically possible.

**Varoufakis, Y. (2020). *Another Now.***
Dialogic exploration of alternative post-capitalist arrangements. Influences thinking on transition and adoption.

**Frase, P. (2016). *Four Futures.***
Maps the possibility space for what comes after capitalism, given automation and ecology. Useful for situating this framework among alternatives.

**Phillips, L. and Rozworski, M. (2019). *The People's Republic of Walmart.***
The argument that Walmart, Amazon, and other mega-corporations already run planned economies internally. Demolishes the "planning is impossible" objection.

**Albert, M. (2003). *Parecon: Life After Capitalism.***
Participatory economics. Influences thinking on Layer 3 democratic participation and work.

**Benanav, A. (2020). *Automation and the Future of Work.***
The political economy of automation and under-employment. Relevant to the work/post-work sections.

**Stiegler, B. (2010). *For a New Critique of Political Economy.***
The political economy of attention, knowledge, and care. Relevant to framework components not addressed by standard economics.

## Governance and political theory

**Rosanvallon, P. (2008). *Counter-Democracy.***
The role of vigilance, expertise, and distrust in democratic practice. Relevant to the override-body design.

**Landemore, H. (2020). *Open Democracy.***
The case for sortition-based citizen assemblies. Directly influences the override body structure.

**Fung, A. and Wright, E.O., eds. (2003). *Deepening Democracy.***
Empowered participatory governance. Porto Alegre participatory budgeting and similar experiments. Relevant to Layer 3 practices.

**Sheldon Wolin (2008). *Democracy Incorporated.***
The concept of "managed democracy" and "inverted totalitarianism." Useful for diagnosing current conditions.

**Elster, J. (1998). *Deliberative Democracy.***
The theory of deliberation that underlies citizen-assembly design.

**Dewey, J. (1927). *The Public and Its Problems.***
Dewey's response to Lippmann on whether democracy can function given the complexity of modern society. Foundational.

**Habermas, J. (1992). *Between Facts and Norms.***
The communicative-action framework for democratic legitimacy. Relevant to the framework's transparency commitments.

**Ostrom, E. (1990). *Governing the Commons.***
The foundational work on commons governance at various scales. Shows that commons can be governed without either state or market when specific design principles are met.

**Mearsheimer, J. and Walt, S. (2007). *The Israel Lobby and U.S. Foreign Policy.***
The academic treatment of a specific captured-influence pattern. Named by the author as relevant context.

## AI governance and technology

**Bostrom, N. (2014). *Superintelligence.***
The foundational work on AI existential risk. The framework's stance is that advanced AI is coming and must be governed rather than avoided; framing it under public, transparent governance is part of the risk mitigation.

**Russell, S. (2019). *Human Compatible.***
The alignment problem framed around inverse reward modeling. Relevant to the framework's alignment expectations.

**Bender, E. et al. (2021). "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots."***
The critique of large language models as fundamentally limited. The framework takes this seriously; the coordinator's design accommodates LLM limitations through multi-model cross-auditing.

**Crawford, K. (2021). *Atlas of AI.***
The material and political economy of AI systems. Essential for understanding the stack-capture problem.

**Acemoglu, D. and Johnson, S. (2023). *Power and Progress.***
The argument that technology's benefits depend on the institutional structure around it. Supports the framework's position that AI governance matters more than AI capability.

**Zuboff, S. (2019). *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism.***
The business model underlying attention extraction and data harvesting. Relevant to the externalities the framework proposes to price.

## Historical precedents

### Cybersyn

**Medina, E. (2011). *Cybernetic Revolutionaries.***
Allende's attempt at computer-mediated economic coordination in 1970s Chile. The framework's coordinator has intellectual lineage through Cybersyn, though with modern computational capacity and different governance structure. The lesson from Cybersyn's destruction (by the US-backed Pinochet coup) is that such frameworks face active opposition and need legitimacy and protection strategies.

### Deliberative precedents

**Irish Citizen Assembly on the Eighth Amendment.** The 2016-2018 process that led to the abortion referendum. Demonstrated that sortition-based deliberation on contested issues can produce considered outcomes that survive democratic ratification.

**French Citizens' Climate Convention.** Demonstrated the method can be applied to large, complex policy domains, though with mixed results on implementation.

**Belgian permanent citizen councils.** Ongoing experiment in institutionalized sortition.

**Porto Alegre participatory budgeting.** Decades-long experiment in municipal participatory governance.

**Taiwan's vTaiwan and Polis.** Digital deliberation at scale. Relevant to the instruction-set amendment process.

### Institutional precedents

**Norway's Government Pension Fund Global.** The planetary dividend's closest real-world analog at national scale. Demonstrates that commons-based wealth can be managed as a genuine public asset.

**Alaska Permanent Fund.** A universal dividend model, smaller but well-established.

**German codetermination (Mitbestimmung).** Worker representation on boards. Direct precedent for the framework's chartered-firm proposals.

**Mondragón cooperative network.** Worker-owned firms at scale. Demonstrates that worker ownership can compete in market economies.

**Kerala and Kerala model.** Regional government in India demonstrating that substantial human development is achievable at relatively modest per-capita income with the right institutional commitments. Relevant for thinking about livelihood floor calibration.

**Cuban healthcare.** Despite the regime's many problems, Cuban health outcomes relative to per-capita income demonstrate what decommodified basic healthcare can achieve.

### Internet governance

**Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) model.** Rough consensus and running code. Direct precedent for the instruction-set amendment process.

**ICANN and other multistakeholder internet institutions.** Imperfect but operational models of planetary-scale governance of technical commons.

## Intellectual tradition and worldview

**Georgist tradition.** Henry George's *Progress and Poverty* (1879) and subsequent work on land value capture. Relevant to the planetary fund's capitalization.

**Silvia Federici. *Caliban and the Witch* (2004). *Re-enchanting the World* (2018).** The feminist political economy critique that informs the framework's attention to care work and the commons.

**bell hooks.** The interconnection of domination systems (capitalism, patriarchy, racism). Relevant to a framework that must not reproduce them.

**Vandana Shiva.** Critique of agricultural industrialization and corporate control of food systems. Relevant to Layer 1 food security and ecological commitments.

**David Graeber. *Debt: The First 5,000 Years.* (2011). *Bullshit Jobs.* (2018).** Anthropological context for rethinking work and economic organization.

**Ursula K. Le Guin, *The Dispossessed* (1974).** The best literary exploration of what a non-capitalist society with preserved freedom might actually feel like. Relevant for the Layer 3 imagination.

**Octavia Butler, *Parable of the Sower* / *Parable of the Talents* (1993/1998).** The literary exploration of what happens when existing institutions fail. Useful for thinking about transition conditions.

**Kim Stanley Robinson, *Ministry for the Future* (2020).** Fictional exploration of near-future climate governance with specific policy proposals embedded. Overlaps significantly with the framework's concerns.

## Reading list for new contributors

If you're approaching this framework fresh, the most efficient onboarding path:

**For the diagnosis (why current framework is failing):**
1. Hemenway Falk and Tsoukalas, "The AI Layoff Trap" (2026)
2. Raworth, *Doughnut Economics* (2017)
3. Philippon, *The Great Reversal* (2019)
4. Piketty, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014), especially Parts III-IV

**For the alternative vision:**
1. Phillips and Rozworski, *The People's Republic of Walmart* (2019)
2. Cockshott and Cottrell, *Towards a New Socialism* (1993)
3. Bastani, *Fully Automated Luxury Communism* (2019)

**For governance and deliberation:**
1. Landemore, *Open Democracy* (2020)
2. Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (1990)
3. Medina, *Cybernetic Revolutionaries* (2011)

**For strategic orientation:**
1. Friedman's 1982 preface to *Capitalism and Freedom*
2. MacLean, *Democracy in Chains* (2017)
3. Klein, *The Shock Doctrine* (2007) — understanding how frameworks actually get adopted in crisis, including the negative case

**For critique and failure modes:**
1. Scott, *Seeing Like a State* (1998) — on how high-modernist planning fails
2. Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019) — on what happens when AI systems optimize for the wrong thing
3. Acemoglu and Johnson, *Power and Progress* (2023) — on why technology without good institutions produces worse outcomes
