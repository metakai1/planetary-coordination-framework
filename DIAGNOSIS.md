# Diagnosis

## The claim

The free-market framework was coherent under conditions that no longer exist. Continuing to use it is like navigating by a map of a continent that has since shifted. The system is no longer producing the outcomes it was theorized to produce — it is producing the opposite.

This document makes that case in detail. It is the analytical foundation on which everything else in the framework rests. If this diagnosis is wrong, the prescription is wrong.

## Five broken assumptions

Classical and neoclassical economics rest on assumptions that held reasonably well in the 18th through mid-20th centuries and have since been eroded or inverted. Each of the five below is documented in mainstream economics literature; the framework's novelty is not in identifying any single one but in taking them together as a unified pattern requiring structural, not marginal, response.

### 1. Scale ate the small-producer assumption

Adam Smith assumed small producers, local markets, and moral sentiment among proprietors as a check on exploitation. Hayek's case for market coordination rested on the distributed nature of knowledge among many small actors.

Both assumptions have been inverted. Modern firms are larger than most states by revenue. Amazon, Walmart, and large Chinese state-owned enterprises run internal planned economies at scales Gosplan never achieved. Philippon's *The Great Reversal* (2019) documents the collapse of competitive market structure across most US sectors into 3–4 firm oligopolies. The "market" in the Smith sense — many small buyers and sellers, each with negligible individual influence on price — does not exist in most sectors of advanced economies.

**The implication:** within firms, planning already won. The question is whether that planning is democratic or oligarchic, transparent or opaque. "Markets vs. plans" is a resolved debate; only the ownership structure of the plans remains open.

### 2. Information technology ate the calculation argument

Hayek's most powerful case against socialism (1945) was the calculation problem: no central authority could aggregate distributed price information fast enough to match market coordination. Against 1945 computers, this was correct.

Cockshott and Cottrell (*Towards a New Socialism*, 1993) demonstrated mathematically that the calculation problem becomes tractable with modern computational capacity. Daniel Saros extended the work. Real-time planetary-scale optimization now exists — Amazon reprices millions of SKUs continuously, Uber clears a multi-million-participant labor market in seconds, financial markets execute in microseconds. The question is no longer whether planning at scale is computationally feasible; it is whether the resulting systems are democratically accountable or privately captured.

**The implication:** the technological argument for markets over coordination is obsolete. The remaining arguments are political and philosophical, not computational.

### 3. Externalities ate the efficient-market claim

A market is only efficient when prices contain the truth. Most prices don't. Climate, biodiversity, groundwater, topsoil, attention, trust, epistemic commons, pandemic risk, financial contagion, and — per Hemenway Falk and Tsoukalas (2026) — even aggregate consumer demand itself are all commons that private actors deplete while bearing only a fraction of the cost.

The demand externality case is particularly illuminating because it cannot be waved away as a niche environmental concern: it shows that *even firms' own economic interests* are systematically harmed by competitive dynamics they cannot individually escape. Rational firms with perfect foresight race each other off a demand cliff because each captures the full cost saving from automation but bears only 1/N of the aggregate demand destruction. The paper demonstrates that UBI, capital income taxes, worker equity, and Coasian bargaining all fail to correct this. Only a Pigouvian tax closes the wedge.

Generalize the result: **every major civilizational catastrophe of the last fifty years is an unpriced externality.** Carbon pricing remains partial and geographically fragmented. Attention extraction is unpriced entirely. Financial contagion externalities are priced only after the fact, if at all. The framework's core prescription follows from this directly: systematic externality pricing across all planetary commons, with revenue recycled into the commons or distributed to those harmed.

### 4. Financialization ate the productive-investment story

The theoretical case for capital markets rests on their allocation of savings to productive investment. The empirical reality of the last three decades has been the opposite: stock buybacks routinely exceed R&D spending across the S&P 500; most "investment" is secondary-market asset churning rather than capital formation; private equity has demonstrated a reliable pattern of extracting value from productive firms and leaving them hollowed; real capital formation as a share of GDP has declined across the developed world.

The consequence is that concentrated financial power now operates at a scale where it is largely decoupled from the real economy it theoretically serves. BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street hold controlling positions across most of the S&P 500 — meaning a handful of asset managers have effective voting power over the productive capacity of the US economy, without any democratic accountability and largely without direct productive knowledge.

This is not a moral critique of individuals. It is a structural observation: the capital-allocation function that markets were theorized to perform has drifted away from the activity that creates real value.

### 5. Consolidation ate the competition story

Related to but distinct from scale. Even where multiple firms exist, effective competition has collapsed in most sectors through common ownership (same institutional investors own all major competitors), platform dynamics (network effects produce winner-take-most outcomes), regulatory capture (incumbents write the rules), and acquisition (startups are acquired before they become threats).

The result is that consumer-welfare improvements that were supposed to flow from competition increasingly do not. Prices rise, quality degrades, choice narrows, and the "discipline" of competition becomes theoretical.

## The compounding social and cognitive diagnosis

The economic analysis above is necessary but not sufficient. A framework that addresses only economic structure will fail to account for why the population that would need to democratically choose an alternative is not, at the moment, capable of doing so.

Several strands of evidence, each from mainstream sources:

**Health and cognition.** Endocrine-disrupting chemicals (BPA, phthalates, PFAS) are now documented in human bloodstreams and placentas at scale. Microplastics are pervasive. Air particulate pollution is estimated by WHO to contribute to approximately 7 million premature deaths annually. Rising rates of metabolic disease, autoimmune conditions, and neurodevelopmental disorders correlate with environmental toxin loads, though causal mechanisms remain under research.

**Attention and mental health.** Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024), Jean Twenge's longitudinal work, and CDC data document a sharp decline in adolescent mental health coinciding with smartphone and social media adoption. Tristan Harris and the Center for Humane Technology document the deliberate engineering of attention capture for engagement metrics. The attention economy is, in Hemenway Falk's terms, another unpriced externality — with cognition as the commons being depleted.

**Social fabric.** Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone* (2000) and its updates document the decades-long decline of civic participation, community organizations, and social trust across the developed world. Atomization is not a vibe; it is measured.

**Institutional trust.** The Edelman Trust Barometer documents trust collapse across governments, media, and large institutions in most democracies. This is not conspiracy theory — it is the institutions' own measurement.

**Manufactured consent.** Bernays invented modern PR to shape opinion. MKULTRA was declassified and documented (Church Committee, 1975). Operation Mockingbird is a matter of historical record. Cambridge Analytica demonstrated psychographic targeting at scale. The 2012 Smith-Mundt Modernization Act legalized domestic US government psychological operations. Media concentration has proceeded alongside economic concentration. These are not speculative claims.

**Epistemic capture.** The Flexner Report (1910) and Rockefeller/Carnegie foundation funding reshaped American medical education around allopathic, pharmaceutical-intensive models — Paul Starr's Pulitzer-winning *The Social Transformation of American Medicine* is the standard account. Similar funding-driven shaping of economics curricula, think tanks, and policy institutes has been documented by Nancy MacLean, Jane Mayer, and others. The ideas that seem natural in a given era are shaped substantially by who funds their promotion.

**Political capture.** Mearsheimer and Walt's *The Israel Lobby and U.S. Foreign Policy* (2007) is the academic-register treatment of AIPAC's influence. Lee Drutman's *The Business of America is Lobbying* documents corporate capture of US politics. Gilens and Page (2014) published the most-cited empirical finding: US policy outcomes track elite preferences, not median-voter preferences, to a statistically significant degree. The US is, in the narrow academic sense of that paper, an oligarchy.

## The synthesis

The economic structure and the cognitive/social structure reinforce each other. A population that is atomized, distracted, anxious, sick, and epistemically captured cannot democratically restructure the economic system that is producing those conditions. Meanwhile, the economic system concentrates the resources needed to maintain the cognitive and social conditions that protect it. It is a stable, self-reinforcing equilibrium.

Breaking it requires:

1. **Structural intervention.** The system will not be voted out by the population it has debilitated. Some external force — crisis, technology, or coordinated counter-institution — must shift the equilibrium.
2. **Alternative institutions ready in advance.** When the structural shift comes, whatever coherent alternatives are lying around will be adopted. This is how neoliberalism replaced Keynesianism in the 1970s. The right-wing case was ready; the alternatives weren't.
3. **A theory of governance that doesn't assume a functional demos at the start.** Because there isn't one. The framework has to bootstrap democratic self-governance from a starting condition where it doesn't exist, rather than assuming it as a premise.

This is what the remaining documents attempt to specify.

## What this diagnosis is not

It is not a claim that every previous analysis of capitalism is right, or that the current system is unprecedented in its corruption, or that no institutions work. Hospitals still heal. Bridges still stand. Most economies still deliver most of what they deliver. The claim is narrower and more specific: **the coordination mechanisms the system relies on to self-correct have been compromised, and the feedback loops that should produce adaptive response have been severed or inverted.** The framework is a response to that specific failure.

It is also not a claim that coordination is inherently good or that markets are inherently bad. The framework preserves markets in the free middle layer precisely because they remain the best-known mechanism for coordinating diverse preferences at small and medium scale. The diagnosis is that markets fail at *planetary* scale, for the specific structural reasons enumerated above, not that markets fail generally.
