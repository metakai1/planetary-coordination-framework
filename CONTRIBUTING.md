# Contributing

The framework is open to contribution from anyone. This document describes how to propose changes, what kinds of contributions are most valuable, and the norms under which contributors are expected to engage.

## The model: rough consensus and running code

The framework adopts the governance model that built the internet: the **IETF style**. No voting, no formal sovereignty. Proposals are made in the open, debated on their merits, and adopted when they achieve sufficient rough consensus among engaged participants and when they work in practice.

This model has limitations — it privileges existing participants over newcomers, it can produce gatekeeping, and it requires sustained attention to function. But it has the substantial virtue of producing durable, interoperable, widely-adopted standards across a planet-scale network of actors with very different interests. It's the best working model available for what this project is trying to do.

## Types of contributions

Most valuable:

### Substantive disagreement

If you think the framework is wrong — in whole or in part — your contribution is more valuable than someone who agrees. Open an issue that specifies:
- Which claim or proposal you disagree with
- Your reasoning
- What you think should replace it
- What evidence or argument supports your alternative

Substantive, well-reasoned disagreement strengthens the framework by either producing better proposals or exposing weaknesses that need to be acknowledged. Pure agreement teaches us nothing.

### Specialist knowledge

The framework was drafted by a generalist and needs specialist refinement across many domains:
- **Economics:** dividend calibration, externality pricing methodology, transition mechanics, monetary design, interaction with existing financial systems
- **Political theory:** override body design, legitimacy under conditions of contested sovereignty, relationship to existing states
- **AI governance:** stack capture solutions, federated model design, alignment under multi-model cross-auditing
- **Ecology and Earth sciences:** Layer 1 constraint specification and operationalization
- **History:** failure modes of past transitional governance, precedents for the framework's components
- **Law:** constitutional design, the interpretation layer, international legal integration
- **Public health and cognitive science:** operationalizing the "cognitive recovery" expectation
- **Deliberation science:** high-quality citizen assembly design at scale
- **Specific regional and cultural knowledge:** how the framework plays in contexts outside the North American/European perspective in which it was drafted

If you have specialist expertise in any of these domains, your targeted contribution is worth more than broad commentary.

### Critique from underrepresented perspectives

The framework's initial author is a North American developer. The framework will be missing important concerns visible from other standpoints. Particularly welcome:
- Global South perspectives on whether "planetary coordination" reproduces colonial dynamics
- Indigenous scholars on governance traditions not integrated into the framework
- Feminist political economy on care work and reproductive labor
- Disability justice on whether the "dignified life" baseline is genuinely universal
- Ecological economics on whether the framework remains too anthropocentric
- Religious and spiritual traditions on dimensions not addressed

These are expected to surface significant gaps. They are treated as contributions, not threats.

### Translations and communication

The framework's success depends on being accessible across languages, cultural contexts, and levels of specialist knowledge. Contributions to translation and to plain-language presentation are valuable.

### Implementation proposals

Concrete proposals for piloting specific components — municipal livelihood floors, regional citizen assemblies, sector-specific externality pricing, open-stack AI governance experiments — are the path from framework to reality.

## What kinds of contributions are less useful

**Broad endorsement without specifics.** "This is great, you should keep going" doesn't help. What specifically works?

**Broad rejection without alternatives.** "This will never work" doesn't help. Why specifically, and what should replace it?

**Ideological purity claims.** The framework is deliberately agnostic between several political traditions that can find themselves in it. Arguments that it's not adequately Marxist/anarchist/liberal/conservative are less useful than arguments about whether specific components work or fail.

**Conspiracy-theoretic additions.** The framework is built on documented history and citable research. Adding unfalsifiable claims about specific cabals, demographics, or hidden actors weakens rather than strengthens it. The documented structural critique is sufficient.

## How to propose changes

### Issues

Open an issue when you want to raise a concern, disagreement, or question without proposing specific text. Issues are for:
- Pointing out problems
- Asking questions about intent or scope
- Starting conversations about potentially needed changes
- Reporting gaps or inconsistencies

Good issues are specific, evidenced, and invite engagement.

### Pull requests

Open a PR when you have specific text to propose. PRs are for:
- Text changes to existing documents
- New sections within existing documents
- New documents that fit the framework's scope
- Corrections, refinements, and clarifications

Good PRs include:
- Clear description of what's changing and why
- Engagement with the reasoning of the existing text (agreement, disagreement, extension)
- Citations or evidence where relevant
- Willingness to iterate based on discussion

### RFCs (Requests for Comment)

For larger proposals — new major sections, framework extensions, alternative approaches to existing components — open an RFC. An RFC is a longer document that:
- Presents the proposal in full
- Anticipates objections and addresses them
- Cites relevant work
- Specifies what would need to change in the framework to adopt it

RFCs invite extended discussion before specific text changes. Good RFCs change the direction of the framework; weak RFCs get folded into narrower PRs.

## Engagement norms

**Good faith.** Assume contributors are arguing for what they believe, not from hidden motives. Attribute disagreement to different analysis, not to bad character.

**Evidence over assertion.** Claims about how the world works should be supported by evidence. Values and priorities can be stated as values; empirical claims need grounding.

**Specificity.** General critiques are less useful than specific ones. If you disagree with "the coordinator handles planetary commons," say which commons shouldn't be included or which should be added.

**Charity to text.** Read the framework as written, not as you expect it to be. If the text says X and you argue against Y, that's not useful.

**Acknowledgment of update.** If you change your mind based on discussion, say so. This is strength, not weakness, and it models how the framework itself expects to evolve.

**No personal attacks.** Disagree with ideas, not people. The framework is strengthened by sharp disagreement about substance; it is weakened by personal conflict that drives contributors away.

**Patience.** Good proposals get refined over multiple iterations. Frustration with slow progress is understandable but not productive.

## What the framework won't accept

**Attempts to weaponize the framework.** The framework is for planetary coordination around commons, livelihood, and freedom. Contributions that would turn it into a tool for specific nationalist, authoritarian, or oppressive projects will be rejected regardless of how well-argued.

**Violations of the identity-defining commitments.** The sunset clause, the open stack requirement, the Layer 3 freedom commitment, override authority, and wealth-concentration limits are the framework's identity. Proposals that would remove any of these change the framework into something else and won't be accepted as amendments to this framework. You are free to fork the repository and build that other thing.

**Contributions that depend on specific unfalsifiable claims.** See "conspiracy-theoretic additions" above.

## Governance of this repository

During the framework's early phase (while the initiating author is still active), substantive direction is coordinated by the initiating author with input from contributors. This is honest acknowledgment that the framework needs a single coherent drafter in its infancy.

As the framework matures, governance is expected to distribute — working groups for specific components, a steering committee for overall direction, a clear amendment process for foundational changes. The exact structure is itself an [open question](OPEN_QUESTIONS.md) and will be settled through the early contributors' collective decision.

The framework is licensed under CC0 (public domain). Anyone can fork it, extend it, rewrite it, or build on it without permission. The repository's role is coordination, not ownership.
