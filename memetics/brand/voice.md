# Voice

The language rules for all memetic content in this project.

## The register

The framework speaks like a **civic document written by someone who ships**. Serious but not academic. Declarative but not strident. Engineered but not cold. Critical but not cynical.

The models to calibrate against, in descending order of influence:

1. **Technical specifications** (RFCs, IETF documents, arXiv abstracts) — precision, no decoration, every word load-bearing
2. **Constitutional prose** (the US Bill of Rights, the French Declaration, Paine's *Common Sense*) — declarative, unhedged, speaks from principle
3. **Serious journalism** (*The London Review of Books*, *n+1*, *The Baffler*) — willing to have a point of view, allergic to both jargon and cutesiness
4. **Good open-source README files** (the best ones have a voice without performing)

The models to explicitly **avoid**:

- TED talk ("imagine a world where...")
- LinkedIn thought-leadership ("three lessons from...")
- Activist rally ("we the people demand...")
- Academic ("it has been argued that some have suggested perhaps...")
- Corporate ("we're on a mission to empower...")
- Terminally online ("cope harder, billionaires")

## Rules

### 1. Declarative mood is the default

```
YES:  The market cannot price what it cannot see.
NO:   Isn't it time we asked whether the market can price what it cannot see?
```

No questions unless the question is load-bearing. No hedging.

### 2. Short. Very short. Cut again.

If a meme line is more than twelve words, cut. If the subordinate clause is doing work, make it a separate line. If it's not doing work, delete it.

```
YES:  Demand is a commons. Stop depleting it.
NO:   Demand itself functions as a kind of commons that competitive dynamics are depleting at scale.
```

### 3. No exclamation marks

Ever. The intensity comes from the content, not the punctuation. An exclamation mark says *I don't trust my line to land on its own*.

### 4. No hashtags inside the image

Hashtags go in the post, never the graphic. The graphic is a document; the post is the packaging.

### 5. No emoji, no icons, no decorative symbols

See typography.md rule 4.

### 6. Use specific nouns, not general ones

```
YES:  The calculation problem was solved. Privately.
NO:   Many economic problems are being addressed behind closed doors.
```

Specifics are how you signal you know what you're talking about.

### 7. Claim, don't plead

```
YES:  Exit capitalism. The plan is open source.
NO:   Please consider joining us in exploring alternatives to capitalism.
```

The framework doesn't recruit. It reports.

### 8. No sarcasm except when explicitly adopting the adversary's voice

Sarcasm used in earnest sounds insecure. Sarcasm used to *quote and invert* the opposing position can work — once per composition, maximum.

```
ALLOWED:  When Walmart plans an economy, it's logistics. When we plan one, it's tyranny. Interesting.
```

### 9. Never explain the meme

Memes that explain themselves are dead on arrival. Trust the reader to do the inferential work. The ones who get it will get it; the ones who don't will look it up or scroll past. Both outcomes are acceptable.

### 10. Address nobody in particular

The framework doesn't say "you" or "we" or "they." It describes the situation. This is the difference between propaganda (which enlists) and specification (which states).

```
YES:  Scarcity is a distribution problem.
NO:   You've been told scarcity is real. It isn't.
```

## The italic emphasis word

One word per composition may carry italic-verdigris emphasis. Choose the load-bearing word — the one that, if removed, collapses the line's meaning.

```
Demand is a commons.               (emphasis on "commons" — the conceptual reframe)
The plan is open source.           (emphasis on "open" — the political claim)
Markets at planetary scale         (emphasis on "planetary" — the category error)
  is a category error.
```

Not every composition has an emphasis word. Some lines are stronger flat.

## Tone across formats

- **Cards** (1:1, feed format): single-line punch, maximum restraint
- **Posters** (portrait, print-ready): can carry more text — one headline plus 2-3 supporting lines
- **Banners** (16:9 landscape): quiet and wide, often just a line plus the URL
- **Stencils** (high-contrast SVG): bumper-sticker energy, four words or fewer

Register stays constant across formats. Only information density changes.

## Approved source language

When a line is adapted from a source, the source matters. Acceptable sources:

- Direct compressions from the framework's own documents
- Paraphrases of specific findings from cited research (Hemenway Falk, Piketty, Philippon, etc.)
- References to canonical thinkers the audience will recognize (Friedman, Hirschman, Keynes, Ostrom, Hayek)
- Rephrasings of traditional political/civic language (rights-of-man style, constitutional style)

Unacceptable sources:

- Specific living politicians' rhetoric (dates the work and locks it to a cycle)
- Meme templates from Twitter/X (dates the work to weeks)
- Advertising copy tropes ("tired of X? Try Y!")
- Self-help language ("unlock your", "transform your")

## Final test

Before publishing any line, read it aloud. If it sounds like:

- A founder pitching — cut or rewrite
- A politician campaigning — cut or rewrite
- A professor lecturing — cut or rewrite
- A coach motivating — cut or rewrite

If it sounds like a sentence in a specification document, a clause in a treaty, or a line in a book that might still be worth reading in fifty years — ship it.
